# Day 24: Company-Specific Deep Preparation

## Make Yourself the Obvious Choice for Each Company

Generic preparation gets you in the door. Company-specific preparation gets you the offer. Today you'll learn to research companies deeply and position yourself as the perfect fit for their unique needs.

---

## Why Company-Specific Preparation Matters

### The Reality of Hiring

Companies don't hire "good engineers." They hire engineers who can solve **their specific problems**.

**Generic candidate:**
"I'm a senior backend engineer with 8 years of experience in Java and distributed systems."

**Company-specific candidate:**
"I've spent 5 years building microservices at scale—exactly what you're doing with your migration from the Rails monolith. I led a similar migration at my current company and can share specific patterns that worked for your use case."

**Who gets the offer?** The second candidate, every time.

---

### What Companies Really Want

**Tech Lead/Architect Expectations by Company Type:**

**Startup (Series A):**
- Move fast, ship features
- Comfortable with ambiguity
- Willingness to wear many hats
- Pragmatic over perfect
- **Architect focus**: Make build vs buy decisions quickly, balance technical debt

**Startup (Series C/D):**
- Scale systems for growth
- Build team and processes
- Balance speed with quality
- Mentor junior engineers
- **Architect focus**: Design for 10x scale, establish engineering standards, technical strategy

**Big Tech (FAANG):**
- Solve complex technical challenges
- Collaborate across large organizations
- Navigate ambiguity at scale
- Drive initiatives with broad impact
- **Architect focus**: Cross-team influence, platform thinking, technical vision for org

**Enterprise:**
- Deep domain expertise
- Work within constraints (legacy, compliance)
- Partner with business stakeholders
- Long-term thinking and planning
- **Architect focus**: Modernization strategy, migration roadmaps, governance frameworks

**Your job:** Position yourself for the company's stage and needs.

## Researching Target Companies

### Phase 1: Company Fundamentals (30 minutes)

**Company Website:**
- About page: Mission, values, history
- Products page: What they build, who they serve
- Team page: Leadership, culture, team size
- Careers page: What they value in candidates

**LinkedIn:**
- Company page: Recent updates, employee count growth
- Employees: Team composition, backgrounds
- Your interviewers: Research who's interviewing you
- Job posting: Read carefully, note specific requirements

**Crunchbase/TechCrunch:**
- Funding stage and investors
- Growth trajectory
- Recent news and announcements
- Competitive landscape

**Glassdoor:**
- Interview questions candidates reported
- Company culture insights
- Compensation ranges
- Common complaints (prepare to address)

**Key questions to answer:**
- What stage is the company? (early startup, scaling, mature)
- What's their primary product or service?
- Who are their customers? (B2B, B2C, enterprise, consumer)
- What's their business model? (SaaS, marketplace, ads, transaction fees)
- How are they growing? (revenue, users, team size)
- What are their technical challenges? (scale, legacy, greenfield)

---

### Phase 2: Engineering Blog Deep Dive (1-2 hours)

**Most valuable research source:** The company's engineering blog.

**What to look for:**

**1. Technical Stack:**
- Languages and frameworks
- Databases and data stores
- Infrastructure (cloud provider, orchestration)
- Monitoring and observability tools

**2. Architecture Patterns:**
- Microservices vs. monolith
- Event-driven architecture
- API design patterns
- Data pipeline architecture

**3. Engineering Challenges:**
- Scale problems they've solved
- Migrations they've completed
- Performance optimizations
- Reliability improvements

**4. Engineering Culture:**
- How they make decisions
- How they approach testing
- How they handle incidents
- What they value (speed, quality, innovation)

**5. Recent Initiatives:**
- What they're working on now
- Technology experiments
- Open source contributions
- Developer tools they're building

### Phase 3: GitHub Repository Review (1 hour)

**If the company has public repositories:**

**What to analyze:**

**1. Code Quality:**
- Code style and conventions
- Test coverage
- Documentation quality
- Code review comments (if open source)

**2. Technology Choices:**
- Languages and frameworks
- Third-party libraries
- Development tools and setup

**3. Contribution Patterns:**

- How many contributors?

**4. Architecture Insights:**

- Data models and schemas

**How to use this:**
- Reference specific repos in your interview: "I looked at your [repo name] and noticed you use [technology]..."
- Ask informed questions: "I saw in your [repo] you're using [pattern]. How has that worked for you?"
- Demonstrate understanding: "Based on your open source work, it seems you value [quality/speed/innovation]..."

---

### Phase 4: Product Deep Dive (1-2 hours)

**Use the product extensively before your interview.**

**For B2C products:**
- Sign up as a user
- Try all major features
- Note pain points
- Identify opportunities

**For B2B products:**
- Request a demo
- Read documentation
- Watch product videos
- Understand the value proposition

**Questions to consider:**
- What makes this product unique?
- Who are the competitors?
- What user problems does it solve?
- What's the user experience like?
- Where could it improve?

**How to use this:**
- Show genuine interest: "I've been using your product for the past week, and I love [specific feature]..."
- Offer insights: "As a user, I noticed [pain point]. Have you considered [solution]?"
- Demonstrate understanding: "I can see how [feature] would be valuable for [user persona]..."

**Warning:** Don't overdo criticism. Frame feedback as curiosity:
- Good: "I'm curious how you approach [challenge]. I noticed [observation]..."
- Bad: "Your product is slow and the UX is confusing."

### Phase 5: Competitive Landscape (30 minutes)

**Understand the company's position in the market:**

**Direct competitors:**
- Who are the top 3 competitors?
- What are their strengths?
- What differentiates this company?

**Indirect competitors:**
- What alternative solutions exist?
- Why do customers choose this product?

**Market trends:**
- Is the market growing or shrinking?

**How to use this:**

- Demonstrate interest: "I've been following the [industry] space, and it's exciting to see how you're approaching [problem]..."
- Ask strategic questions: "Where do you see the market going in the next 3-5 years?"

## Tech Stack Alignment and Preparation

### Matching Your Experience to Their Stack

**Step 1: Identify their stack**
- From job posting
- From engineering blog
- From GitHub repos
- From product research (view source, network tab)

**Step 2: Map to your experience**

**Direct match:**
"You use PostgreSQL. I've worked with PostgreSQL for 4 years, including optimizing queries for a 5TB database."

**Transferable experience:**
"You use Cassandra. I haven't used Cassandra, but I have deep experience with distributed databases—I worked with DynamoDB for 3 years and understand consistency trade-offs, partition strategies, and operational challenges."

**No match but willing to learn:**
"You use Rust. I haven't used Rust professionally, but I have strong systems programming experience in C++ and I've been learning Rust in my personal projects. I'm excited about the memory safety guarantees and performance characteristics."

---

### Preparing for Stack-Specific Questions

**If they use a technology you know:**
- Prepare deep technical questions
- Review advanced features
- Recall specific experiences
- Prepare to discuss trade-offs

**Example: They use React**
- Review latest React features (hooks, concurrent mode, suspense)
- Prepare stories about React performance optimization
- Discuss component architecture patterns you've used
- Be ready to compare React to alternatives (Vue, Angular, Svelte)

---

**If they use a technology you don't know:**
- Learn the basics (syntax, core concepts)
- Understand why it's popular
- Relate it to technologies you know
- Show willingness to learn

**Example: They use Go, you know Python**
- Learn Go basics (goroutines, channels, interfaces)
- Understand why they might have chosen Go (performance, concurrency, simplicity)
- Relate to your Python experience: "Go's goroutines are similar to Python's async/await but with better performance..."
- Show interest: "I've been impressed with Go's simplicity and performance. I've started working through [Go tutorial/book]..."

### How to Reference GitHub Repos in Interview

**Good:**
"I explored your [repo name] repository and was impressed by your testing approach. I noticed you use [pattern/tool]. I've used a similar approach where [example]. I'm curious—how has that worked for you at scale?"

**Good:**
"I saw in your open-source [project] that you're using [technology]. I have experience with [similar technology] and I'm excited to learn [new technology]. What's been your experience with it?"

**Bad:**
"I looked at your GitHub but I don't understand why you did [thing]. It seems wrong."

**Bad:**
"Your code has a lot of bugs. Here's everything you should fix..."

**Approach:** Curious and collaborative, not judgmental.

## Understanding Business Domain

### Why Domain Knowledge Matters

**Surface-level candidate:**
"I can build any backend system. Technology is technology."

**Domain-aware candidate:**
"I understand that in fintech, transaction consistency and audit trails are critical. I've built similar systems with idempotent APIs, event sourcing for audit trails, and careful handling of money with decimal precision."

**Who gets the offer?** The domain-aware candidate.

---

### Key Domains and Their Unique Challenges

**Fintech:**
- Transaction consistency (money can't be lost)
- Audit trails (regulatory compliance)
- Security (PCI compliance, fraud prevention)
- Decimal precision (no floating point for money!)

**Healthcare:**
- Privacy (HIPAA compliance)
- Legacy system integration (HL7, FHIR)
- Reliability (lives depend on it)
- Data accuracy (medical records)

**E-commerce:**
- Scale (traffic spikes during sales)
- Inventory consistency (overselling prevention)
- Payment integration (PCI compliance)
- Personalization and recommendations

**Social media:**
- Extreme scale (millions of concurrent users)
- Real-time updates (feeds, notifications)
- Content moderation (safety)
- Recommendation algorithms

**Gaming:**
- Low latency (real-time multiplayer)
- Cheat prevention
- Matchmaking algorithms
- Global distribution (low latency worldwide)

**Enterprise SaaS:**
- Multi-tenancy (data isolation)
- Customization (per-customer configs)
- SSO and authentication (enterprise requirements)
- Uptime and reliability (SLAs)

---

### Preparing Domain-Specific Stories

**Research the domain:**
- Read industry blogs and news
- Understand common challenges
- Learn domain terminology
- Study regulatory requirements

**Adapt your stories:**
- Emphasize relevant experiences
- Use domain terminology
- Show understanding of constraints
- Demonstrate awareness of trade-offs

**Example: Interviewing for fintech**

**Generic story:**
"I built an API that processes 10K requests per second with 99.9% uptime."

**Domain-adapted story:**
"I built a payment processing API that handles 10K transactions per second with exactly-once semantics. We used idempotency keys and event sourcing to ensure no duplicate charges, and we implemented comprehensive audit trails for compliance. We achieved 99.9% uptime with automatic failover to secondary regions within 60 seconds."

**Notice:** Exactly-once, idempotency, event sourcing, audit trails, compliance—all fintech-relevant terms.

## Tailoring Your Experience to Company Needs

### Step 1: Analyze the Job Posting

**Read the job posting carefully:**
- Required skills
- Nice-to-have skills
- Responsibilities
- Project examples
- Team description

**Annotate the posting:**
- Highlight keywords
- Note technologies mentioned
- Identify priorities (what's listed first?)
- Spot patterns in requirements

---

### Step 2: Map Your Experience

**Create a two-column table:**

| Their Need | My Experience |
|------------|---------------|
| "Scale backend to handle 10M users" | Scaled API from 100K to 5M requests/day |
| "Microservices experience" | Led migration from monolith to 12 microservices |
| "Mentoring junior engineers" | Mentored 3 junior engineers, 2 promoted to mid-level |
| "React and TypeScript" | 4 years React, 2 years TypeScript |

---

### Step 3: Prioritize Stories

**For each requirement, prepare:**
- 1 detailed story (STAR format)
- 2-3 brief examples
- Metrics and outcomes
- Lessons learned

**Example: "Experience scaling systems"**

**Detailed story:**
"At [Company], I led the effort to scale our API from 100K to 5M requests per day. We started seeing performance degradation at 500K requests/day—response times went from 200ms to 3 seconds. I led a 3-month project that included database query optimization, implementing Redis caching, and horizontally scaling our app servers. We reduced p95 response time to 150ms even at 5M requests/day. This directly enabled our revenue growth from $5M to $25M ARR."

**Brief examples:**
- Optimized N+1 queries reducing database load 80%
- Implemented CDN reducing image load time 90%
- Designed auto-scaling strategy saving $50K/year in infrastructure costs

---

### Step 4: Frame Your Experience

**Use their language:**
- If they say "microservices," use "microservices" (not "distributed systems")
- If they say "event-driven," use "event-driven" (not "messaging")
- If they say "scale," use "scale" (not "performance")

**Quantify impact:**
- Not: "Improved performance"
- Better: "Reduced API latency from 500ms to 50ms"

**Connect to their outcomes:**
- Not: "Built a feature"
- Better: "Built [feature] that increased user engagement 30%"
